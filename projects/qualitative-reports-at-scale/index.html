Qualitative Reports at Scale

UXInternal ToolStartupYou

For a learning program tracking creative capacity and growth - the reports need to be personalised and qualitative, highlighting glow and grow areas for each student with evidence of practice, across multiple simultaneous cohorts. It took 4 weeks to manually compile, condense, design and publish 400 reports between 4 faculty - scaling linearly. The workflow had to be optimised for growth.

The current workflow had two distinct problems. 1. Input quality: faculty observations needed to be specific and evidence-backed for the reports to mean anything. 2. Output volume: even with good notes, transforming them into hundreds of coherent, well-toned, personalised documents with a limited workforce in record time, was a challenge. For input, faculty used a structured format with short, sharp observation notes during sessions, and a mix of pre-defined rubric led scoring and free form notes for submission evaluations. Live sessions for recorded for analysing later if needed. LLM handled the rest: expanding notes into full paragraphs, synthesising observations across multiple sessions into a coherent narrative, and maintaining a tone that was motivating but restrained across every single report. A custom script then pulled faculty inputs, student submission data, and programme-specific metrics through a Google Sheets and Slides template system, visualising the data and generating PDFs in bulk. Faculty has the option of revising a report manually within the system before the final pass is sent for batch conversion to PDF.

The reporting cycle dropped from 4 weeks to 1 week. Reports were more consistent in tone than the ones produced manually, and richer in qualitative depth. Faculty could focus entirely on observation quality without having to worry about writing paragraphs for hundreds of students being mindful of their tone. Each report contained a narrative summary, glow and grow sections with specific evidence, and a visual data layer showing individual progress metrics.

The tool is upstream-dependent in two directions. If student submissions were missing, there was nothing to report on. If faculty had little to observe because of disengaged sessions or low participation then the notes were too thin for even a well-prompted LLM to work with. The response was to redesign parts of the programme itself: lower-barrier quests to generate submission data, and modules like the sci-fi design challenge reworked for more active individual participation. The reporting tool helped us refine the curriculum and make it more accessible and engaging for a wider audience.

Process

Collect Report Inputs

The first step gathers notes, transcripts, themes, and supporting context into one reporting flow so insights are not lost before synthesis even begins.

Notes Themes Quotes Context

Input Layer

A reporting system becomes easier to trust when the source material stays connected to the final readout instead of being flattened away too early.

Traceability

That connection makes it easier for later readers to understand where a finding came from and how much context supports it.

Structure Findings

The middle layer organizes observations into signals, themes, and reusable units that can be scanned quickly across projects.

ThemesSignalsPatternsEvidence

Reading Layer

Structuring the findings makes the report easier to move through, so people can understand the shape of the research without losing important nuance.

Cross-Project Value

Once findings become legible in a shared format, they can start informing more than one project instead of staying trapped inside a single readout.

Share at Scale

The final layer focuses on circulation: helping reports travel across teams, survive handoffs, and remain useful beyond the original presentation moment.

Circulation Layer
